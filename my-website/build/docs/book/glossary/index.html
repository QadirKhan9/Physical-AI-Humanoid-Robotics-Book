Glossary

This glossary provides definitions for key terms used throughout the book on Physical AI and Humanoid Robotics.

A​

Actuator: A component of a robot that converts energy into physical motion. Common types include servo motors, stepper motors, and hydraulic/pneumatic actuators.

Anthropomorphic: Having human-like characteristics in form or behavior. In robotics, refers to robots designed with human-like physical features.

B​

Balance Control: Systems and algorithms that maintain a robot's stability, particularly important for bipedal robots that must maintain equilibrium while walking or standing.

Bipedal Locomotion: The ability to walk on two legs, a key capability for humanoid robots to navigate human environments.

C​

Control Theory: The mathematical study of how to influence the behavior of dynamical systems. Essential for robot motion control and stability.

Cognitive Architecture: The structure and organization of intelligent systems, including how they process information, make decisions, and learn.

D​

Degrees of Freedom (DOF): The number of independent movements a mechanical system can make. A human arm has 7 DOF, providing dexterous manipulation capabilities.

Dynamics: The study of forces and torques that cause motion. In robotics, dynamics models predict how robots move in response to applied forces.

E​

Embodied AI: Artificial intelligence systems that interact with the physical world through sensors and actuators, as opposed to purely digital AI systems.

End Effector: The tool or grasping device at the end of a robotic arm. For humanoid robots, this is typically a hand designed for manipulation tasks.

F​

Forward Kinematics: The process of determining the position and orientation of a robot's end effector based on the joint angles of its arm.

Force Control: Control strategies that regulate the forces applied by a robot, important for safe interaction with humans and delicate objects.

G​

Gait: The pattern of leg movements used for locomotion. For humanoid robots, gait control is essential for stable walking.

Gyroscopic Effect: The tendency of a rotating object to maintain its axis of rotation. Used in balance control for humanoid robots.

I​

Inverse Kinematics: The process of determining the joint angles needed to achieve a desired position and orientation of a robot's end effector.

Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU): A device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body, used for balance and orientation in robots.

K​

Kinematics: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause it. Forward and inverse kinematics are fundamental to robot control.

L​

Locomotion: The ability to move from one place to another. For humanoid robots, this typically refers to walking, running, or climbing.

M​

Manipulation: The ability to handle or control objects with the hands or other end effectors. A key capability for humanoid robots.

Model Predictive Control (MPC): An advanced control method that uses a model of the system to predict future behavior and optimize control actions.

P​

Physical AI: A field that combines artificial intelligence with physical systems, enabling AI systems to interact with and control physical environments.

Perception System: The components of a robot that process sensory information from the environment, including vision, touch, hearing, and proprioception.

R​

Robot Operating System (ROS): A flexible framework for writing robot software, providing services designed for a heterogeneous computer cluster.

Redundancy: In robotics, having more degrees of freedom than necessary to perform a task, providing flexibility in motion planning and obstacle avoidance.

S​

Sensor Fusion: The process of combining data from multiple sensors to improve the accuracy and reliability of environmental perception.

Stability: The ability of a robot to maintain balance and continue operating without falling or losing control.

T​

Torque Control: Control strategies that regulate the rotational force applied by actuators, important for precise motion control in humanoid robots.

Trajectory Planning: The process of determining the path a robot should follow to move from one position to another while satisfying constraints.

W​

Whole-Body Control: Control strategies that coordinate all parts of a humanoid robot simultaneously, considering balance, manipulation, and other tasks together.

This glossary will be expanded as additional terms are introduced throughout the book.
